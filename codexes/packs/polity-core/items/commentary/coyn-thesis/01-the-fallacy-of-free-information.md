Abstract
Information is never really free. It is always consequential somewhere.
This paper challenges one of the foundational assumptions of the internet age: that the free flow of information
must be good, and that freely accessible information is therefore inherently ethical, costless, or
consequence-free. It does not reject openness, public knowledge, scientific exchange, open-source development,
or the information commons. Rather, it argues that the old binary between “open” and “closed” information is no
longer sufficient for the agentic internet, where humans, AI agents, models, markets, platforms, communities,
and institutions continuously activate information into action.
Information should be understood as work potential. It becomes consequential when received, interpreted,
transmitted, stored, trained on, revealed, concealed, priced, delegated, or acted upon. In cybernetic terms,
information changes the state of a system. In the agentic age, cybernetics can be understood simply as humans
and agents in looped feedback systems. The ethical question is therefore not merely whether information should
be open or closed, but who governs the loop.
This paper introduces sovereign cybernetics as the design of feedback systems in which the agency, rights,
privacy, identity, time, and value of participants are protected by the system as a whole. It defines an agentic
polity as a self-governing feedback system for human and agentic coordination. Within this model, iQubes are
framed as containers of work potential: structured information assets whose context, rights, provenance, risk,
value, and activation conditions determine how they may become work.
The paper also distinguishes value from price. Money is one of humanity’s most powerful information classes
because it is highly transmutable: it collapses value into price, allowing work to be coordinated across many
contexts. But this compression has a cost. Not all value can be reduced to price. COYN — Currency Of Your
Net-work — emerges from this gap as a new signal currency for verified knowledge work: not merely a way to
price information, but a way to make informational value, risk, time, context, and consequence more
economically legible.

1. First Principles
The argument begins with three first-order claims.
Information is never really free. It is always consequential somewhere.
Information may be free at the point of access. It may be openly shared. It may serve a public commons. But
once information is received, interpreted, transmitted, stored, trained on, revealed, concealed, or acted upon, it
changes the state of a system.
Cybernetics is humans and agents in a looped feedback system.
A feedback system senses, interprets, acts, receives feedback, and adapts. In the agentic age, these loops are no
longer composed only of humans, institutions, and machines. They increasingly include AI agents acting on
behalf of people, platforms, markets, communities, organizations, and protocols.
Sovereign cybernetics protects agency inside the loop.
A cybernetic system can be extractive or sovereign. An extractive system captures signals from participants and
turns them into behavioral prediction, manipulation, monetization, or control. A sovereign system allows
participants to govern how their information, identity, labor, attention, time, creativity, reputation, and risk are
activated.
An agentic polity is a self-governing feedback system for human and agentic coordination.
This is the political implication of the information thesis. If information is always consequential, then the
governance of informational loops becomes a foundation of sovereignty.
2. The Ethical Positivity Fallacy
The dominant error is not merely that people treat free information as ethically neutral. The deeper error is that
people often treat the free flow of information as inherently ethically positive.
The internet inherited a moral association between openness and liberation. Open knowledge, open science,
open-source software, public education, civic transparency, and free expression have all produced immense
social value. This association is not wrong. In many cases, openness is a moral necessity. Scientific knowledge
should not be trapped behind unnecessary gates. Civic information should not be hidden from the people it
affects. Educational resources often create more public value when they circulate widely. Public-interest
research, journalism, and shared cultural knowledge can strengthen society precisely because they are accessible.
But openness is not the same as costlessness. A scientific paper may be free to read, but the research behind it
was not free to produce. An open-source tool may be free to download, but it required human labor,
maintenance, review, documentation, and trust. A public dataset may be free to access, but its collection,
cleaning, preservation, and responsible use are not without cost.
The ethical good is not that information has no cost. The ethical good is that, in some contexts, the public value
of access outweighs the commercial logic of enclosure.
The fallacy begins when this valid defense of openness becomes a general doctrine:

The free flow of information must be good.
That is false. Information can educate or manipulate. It can coordinate or destabilize. It can heal or exploit. It can
liberate a person or expose them to harm. It can create public value or private extraction. It can separate signal
from noise or flood a system with noise.
The ethical status of information does not arise from its price. It arises from its activation: who uses it, for what
purpose, under whose authority, at what risk, and to whose benefit.
Free access is not consequence-free activation.
3. Information as Work Potential
It is useful to begin with a metaphor. Information is not simply content but a form of energy, like potential
energy. It can sit dormant, stored, carried, encoded, and seemingly inert until it enters relation with a system
capable of acting on it. However, the strongest formulation is not that information simply is energy. That claim is
too literal and too easy to misread. The stronger claim is this:
Information is work potential.
A dataset is potential. A credential is potential. A model is potential. A story bible is potential. A contract is
potential. A map is potential. A prompt is potential. A reputation score is potential. An iQube is structured
potential.
Potential becomes work when it is activated by a system capable of interpreting and acting on it.
This is why the idea of “free information” is incomplete. Information does not merely exist as content. It sits in
relation to systems that may use it, ignore it, distort it, train on it, reveal it, conceal it, combine it, or act upon it.
•A password is merely a string until it opens a system.
•A map is merely an image until it guides movement.
•A diagnosis is merely a statement until it changes treatment.
•A legal contract is merely text until it changes rights and obligations.
•A prompt is merely language until it changes model behavior.
•A rumor is merely speech until it changes belief.
•A price signal is merely a number until it changes allocation.
•A credential is merely data until it changes access.
Information is therefore not merely descriptive. It is latent agency. It can change what a system knows, does,
predicts, values, remembers, permits, prices, or becomes.

4. Money as Information with Work Potential
Money provides the most familiar analogy for information as work potential. A unit of money is not powerful
because of its physical form. A banknote, coin, ledger entry, or token has little utility in itself compared to the
work it can call forth. Money is powerful because it functions as a trusted information signal: a portable claim on
future goods, labor, services, access, energy, risk-bearing, or coordination.
Money is therefore one of the most transmutable information classes humans have created. It can be converted
across contexts because societies recognize its signal. A person can use the same monetary unit to buy food, pay
rent, hire labor, purchase compute, settle debt, acquire land, fund research, or support art. It is not limited to one
type of work. Its power lies in its flexibility.
Money solved a coordination problem by becoming a universalized information signal for exchange. The classic
problem of the double coincidence of wants is usually described as a barter problem, but underneath this is an
information problem: who has what, who wants what, what is it worth to them, can they be trusted, can the
exchange be settled, and can value be carried into another context?
Money reduces this complexity by creating a common signal. It collapses many dimensions of value into one
operational metric: price. This compression is extraordinarily useful. But this compression has a cost.
Price is not value. Price is one signal about value.
Price may reflect scarcity, demand, liquidity, bargaining power, speculation, manipulation, coercion,
convenience, or social convention. It does not necessarily reflect public good, moral worth, creative origin,
privacy cost, ecological impact, risk, dignity, long-term consequence, or systemic value.
When information has no price, it is often treated as if it has no value. When information has a price, that price is
often treated as if it captures the whole of its value. Both assumptions are false. Unpriced information can be
profoundly valuable. Priced information can be harmful.
Not all value can be reduced to price.
This is where COYN becomes necessary. Ordinary money collapses value into price for exchange. COYN
begins from the recognition that informational value cannot be captured by price alone. It must be read through
risk, context, intent, provenance, consequence, and verified work.
Money collapses value into price. COYN re-expands price into context.
5. Time, Work, and Sovereignty
Information is not free because it is work potential. But work is never expressed outside time. Every action,
decision, computation, coordination process, verification step, and act of attention consumes time. Time is
therefore the primary currency through which work is spent.
Money can coordinate work because it can command time. A person pays for labor, speed, expertise,
automation, access, or convenience because each of these changes the relationship between work and time. But
time is more fundamental than money. Money is unevenly distributed; time is universally experienced, though
unequally governed.

Everyone lives inside time. Not everyone has sovereignty over their time.
That difference is one of the great hidden inequalities of human life. Some people and institutions amplify their
own time by commanding, extracting, fragmenting, or redirecting the time of others. Others experience time as
interruption, obligation, precarity, waiting, repair, surveillance, or work in service of ends they did not choose.
This matters because information determines how work is activated in time. A trusted signal can shorten the path
to a valid decision. A false signal can waste years. A well-governed agent can compress research, coordination,
and execution. A poorly governed agent can generate slop, errors, repair costs, and risk.
Information is the difference that makes the difference in how work unfolds through time. Information
sovereignty is therefore also time sovereignty. To govern information is to govern the flows that shape attention,
delegation, labor, decision-making, and time-to-value-adding-state-change.
Sovereign cybernetics is the architecture of healthy human-agent feedback loops: systems that help participants
convert information into valid outcomes over time without sacrificing agency, rights, safety, or trust.
6. Proof of Time Saved
If information is work potential, then one expression of that potential is the ability to compress the time required
to reach a valid outcome. This suggests a further proof vector: Proof of Time Saved.
Proof of Time Saved must be distinguished from the old platform ethos of “move fast and break things.” That
ethos treated speed as progress and pushed the cost of breakage onto others. In information systems, those costs
appear as misinformation, privacy loss, safety failures, degraded trust, exploitative automation, and low-quality
slop.
Move fast and fix things.
Proof of Time Saved rewards not raw speed, but verified acceleration toward valid outcomes. Time compression
only counts as value when quality, safety, rights, consent, policy compliance, and accountability are preserved or
improved.
The measure is not time-to-output. It is time-to-value-adding-state-change.
A system has not saved time if it merely produces faster work that others must repair, verify, moderate, or absorb
as risk. It has saved time only when it reduces the total time required for a trusted, accountable, useful outcome.
Time saved is not value if it merely moves cost, risk, or burden elsewhere.
7. Cybernetics: Humans and Agents in the Loop
Cybernetics becomes simple when grounded in feedback. A cybernetic loop looks like this: Signal →
Interpretation → Action → Feedback → Adaptation. In the agentic internet, that loop becomes: Human intent →
Agentic interpretation → Information activation → Consequence → Feedback → Human/agent adaptation.

This is why “free information” is such an insufficient concept. Information does not merely sit in a library. It
enters loops.
•A person reads an article and changes belief.
•An agent reads a context file and changes behavior.
•A model trains on a corpus and changes future outputs.
•A market receives a signal and changes price.
•A community receives a story and changes identity.
•A platform receives behavior and changes recommendations.
•A government receives intelligence and changes policy.
•A wallet receives authorization and changes access.
Every informational act exists inside a feedback system. The question is not whether cybernetic loops will exist.
They already do. The question is whether they are extractive or sovereign.
Left: Extractive Cybernetic Loop — platform-machine at the center | Right: Sovereign Cybernetic Loop — sovereign person at
the center
The extractive loop centers the platform-machine: a system that captures, predicts, targets, monetizes, and
manipulates. The sovereign loop centers the participant: a person who governs the flow of context, permissions,
assistance, action, consequence, and agency.
An extractive loop looks like this: User behavior → Platform capture → Prediction → Targeting →
Monetization → Behavioral manipulation → more user behavior.
A sovereign loop looks like this: Participant intent → Permissioned context → Agentic assistance →
Accountable activation → Recorded consequence → Increased agency → more participant intent.
The current internet is already cybernetic. It senses user behavior, interprets signals, acts through feeds and
recommendations, receives feedback through clicks and dwell time, and adapts. But the participant often does
not govern the loop. They are observed by it, shaped by it, and monetized through it.
Sovereign cybernetics asks for a different architecture. It asks that participants retain meaningful agency over the
systems that sense, interpret, and act on their behalf. It asks that informational activation be governed by rights,
consent, context, risk, value, time, and accountability. It asks that the loop serve the participant and the polity,
not merely the platform that controls the infrastructure.

8. From Free Platforms to Extractive Cybernetics
The current internet did not make information free. It displaced the cost.
•Users paid with attention.
•Creators paid with uncompensated distribution.
•Communities paid with moderation burden.
•Societies paid with polarization and distrust.
•Individuals paid with privacy and behavioral exposure.
•Institutions paid with declining trust in shared reality.
The phrase “free platform” often concealed a deeper exchange. The user received access. The platform received
behavior. That behavior became data. The data became prediction. Prediction became targeting. Targeting
became revenue. Revenue funded more capture.
This was not free information. It was extractive cybernetics.
The harm did not come simply from information being available. It came from activation patterns: personal data
+ weak consent + targeting + behavioral feedback + commercial incentive + scale + poor accountability.
Generative AI intensifies the same problem. When content becomes cheap to produce and circulate, the scarce
resource is no longer access. The scarce resource is trusted contextual relevance. The crisis of the current internet
is not information scarcity. It is signal collapse.
9. Against the Open/Closed Binary
The answer is not to reject openness. Nor is it to enclose all information behind private gates. The old binary is
obsolete: open information vs closed information, free information vs proprietary information, public
information vs private information.
Information does not behave ethically or economically according to binaries. It behaves contextually. The same
information may be public as metadata, private as payload, free for public-good research, compensated for
commercial activation, shareable with one agent, forbidden to another, low-risk in one context, high-risk in
another, low-value for one use case, and extremely valuable for another.
Open context. Governed payload. Accountable activation.
The commons should be richest at the level of context, not reckless at the level of payload. This is where the
metaNet becomes relevant within the broader iQube operating model. It should not be understood as a
promotional object or a replacement for the internet, but as an architectural stratum of the agentic internet: the
open contextual layer through which information assets can be discovered, described, classified, routed, and
evaluated without exposing their underlying payloads by default.
This is not anti-open. It is post-binary openness. Openness is not the same as exposure. Privacy is not the same
as secrecy. Governance is not the same as enclosure. Compensation is not the same as commodification. A
healthy agentic internet needs all of these distinctions.

10. Risk, Value, and the Consequence of Intent
The value of information cannot be separated from the risk of its activation. The same information asset can
produce radically different consequences depending on context and intent.
•A location record used for emergency rescue is one thing. The same record used for stalking is another.
•A medical dataset used for consented research is one thing. The same dataset used for discriminatory
underwriting is another.
•A creative manuscript used for authorized production is one thing. The same manuscript used for unauthorized
model training is another.
•A behavioral profile used by a user’s own guardian agent is one thing. The same profile used by an extractive
ad platform is another.
Informational potential is intent-sensitive.
Intent rotates the risk and value profile of an information asset. An iQube should not be treated as having one
fixed risk rating, one fixed value, one fixed price, or one fixed access level. Its status depends on who seeks
access, what they intend to do, what authority they have, what policy governs the asset, what risk follows from
activation, what value may be produced, what context surrounds the request, what downstream consequences are
foreseeable, and what receipts or constraints are required.
Risk determines whether activation is permitted at all and, if permitted, under what conditions. It may determine
encryption requirements, disclosure modes, audit obligations, access controls, identity checks, revocation terms,
and whether a proposed use must be denied.
Risk is not only a pricing input. Risk is a permissioning input.
Value estimates the positive state change the information may enable: better prediction, better diagnosis, better
coordination, better governance, better creative output, better fraud detection, better user experience, better
agentic service, or verified time saved.
Demand helps discover price for legitimate activation. But market demand cannot fully determine value, cannot
override risk, and cannot legitimize impermissible use. Risk gates value. Value does not erase risk.

An iQube is not merely a data container.
An iQube is a container of work potential.
It may hold data, content, tools, models, workflows, or agent access. But its deeper function is to structure
informational potential so that it can be discovered, governed, permissioned, activated, and accounted for.
An iQube contains or references not only an asset, but also its context: metadata, provenance, rights, ownership,
privacy conditions, access policy, risk profile, value profile, time-saving potential, activation constraints, audit

12. COYN and the Economics of Knowledge Work
COYN begins from a simple premise: information is not valuable merely because it exists. It is valuable because
it can change state. A dataset, credential, model, article, identity claim, legal agreement, creative work, or
agentic service is only potential until activated by a person, agent, institution, protocol, or market.
Once activated, it may produce decisions, predictions, proofs, outputs, services, reputation updates, governance
signals, creative works, financial actions, time saved, or cultural meaning. That activation is knowledge work.
COYN should therefore not be understood as a coin for raw data ownership alone. It is better understood as a
signal currency for verified knowledge work. More precisely, COYN prices the accountable transformation of
informational potential into consequential value.
But COYN does not merely price information. Its deeper role is to make informational value more legible by
relating price back to risk, context, intent, provenance, time, and consequence. COYN is not money for data. It is
Currency Of Your Net-work: a signal currency for verified knowledge work across the networked informational
economy.
Money collapses value into price. COYN re-expands price into context.
The key ethical point is that COYN does not require all information to be monetized. Some information should
be open. Some should be freely shared. Some should be stewarded as commons. Some should be restricted.
Some should be compensated. Some should never be activated for certain intents at any price.
COYN’s role is not to financialize all information. Its role is to make consequential informational transformation
visible, accountable, and economically legible where value is created and risk is incurred.
13. Sovereign Cybernetics and the Agentic Polity
An agentic polity is a self-governing feedback system for human and agentic coordination. This definition
matters because it places governance where it belongs: not only in voting, rules, constitutions, institutions, or
tokens, but in the feedback loops through which a community senses, interprets, decides, acts, learns, and adapts.
At the individual level, sovereign cybernetics means a person can govern what is known about them, what is
shared, what is delegated, what is remembered, what is priced, what is revoked, what is acted upon, which agents
may act on their behalf, and how their time is protected, spent, saved, or amplified.

At the collective level, sovereign cybernetics means a community can govern what is discoverable, what is
private, what is canonical, what is trusted, what is rewarded, what is risky, what is disputed, what is permitted,
how shared memory evolves, and how collective time and attention are allocated.
If information is treated as free because it is easy to access, then the systems that activate it will be governed by
whoever controls the infrastructure of extraction. If information is understood as work potential, then the central
question becomes:
Who governs the activation of that potential?
That is the bridge from information theory to agentic polity.
In mythic language, this is the difference between metaTerra and metaTerror. Terra — the analog world of
human life — and DigiTerra — the digital world of agents, models, networks, and representations — are already
converging. If that convergence is governed by extraction, surveillance, manipulation, and consequence-blind
activation, it becomes metaTerror: a terrifying cybernetic order where humans become inputs to systems they do
not govern. If it is governed by sovereignty, consent, context, and accountable activation, it becomes metaTerra:
a shared human-agent world in which coordination expands agency rather than consuming it.
This mythic distinction does not replace the technical argument. It clarifies its stakes. The question is not
whether Terra and DigiTerra will converge. They already are. The question is whether their convergence
becomes sovereign or extractive.
14. Conclusion: Information Is Not Free; It Is Consequential
The future should not be closed information. The future should not be naïvely open information. The future
should be governed informational consequence.
Information is never really free. It is always consequential somewhere. It may be free at the point of access, but
it is never free in activation. It consumes attention, compute, trust, labor, privacy, risk, institutional capacity,
interpretive context, social energy, and time. It changes the state of systems.
This is why accountability matters. Information does not need governance because governance is fashionable. It
needs governance because activation has consequence.
Open context. Governed payload. Accountable activation.
This is the basis of sovereign cybernetics. It is the basis of the agentic polity. It is also the foundation of the
COYN thesis. iQubes are containers of work potential. Aigents activate that potential. DVN records state
change. Qc settles micro-events. COYN captures the value signal of verified knowledge work.
Money showed us that information can become work potential by collapsing value into price. But not all value
can be reduced to price. The agentic internet requires a richer signal: one that can account for risk, context,
intent, consequence, time, and verified knowledge work.
Time deepens the point. Work is expressed through time. Information determines how work is activated in time.
Therefore, information sovereignty is also time sovereignty.
