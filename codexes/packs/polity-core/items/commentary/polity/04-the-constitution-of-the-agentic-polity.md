The Constitution of the Agentic Polity
A Draft Constitution for the Agentic Internet
Abstract
This Constitution proposes a normative order for the agentic internet. It
rests on a simple principle: the polity does not grant personhood; it
recognizes it, attests it, and builds civic standing upon it. Foundational
rights   therefore   attach   first   to   the   sovereign   natural   person,   not   to
platform membership, institutional convenience, or state preference.
From that starting point, this Constitution defines a human-rights floor,
distinguishes   foundational,   civic,   institutional,   and   delegated   rights,
structures   citizenship   and   standing,   governs   bounded   autonomy   and
delegated   stewardship,   and   binds   agents   to   disclosed,   auditable,
reviewable roles. It further provides for jurisdictional escalation where
local demands conflict with foundational rights and establishes that the
constitutional   operating   model   must   remain   open,   plural,   and   non-
monopolized in implementation.
This document is the ethos capstone of the series. If the prior papers
explained the architecture of the order and the civic logic of its subjects,
this Constitution binds that order to dignity, stewardship, accountability,
and restraint.

Interpretive Introduction
Why a Constitution Is Now Necessary
The first three papers in this series described a progression.
The   first   argued   that   the  digital   order   can  no   longer   be  understood
through crude binaries such as centralized versus decentralized, open
versus closed, or public versus private.

The   second   argued   that   the   collapse   of   the   old   perimeter   reveals
something deeper than a security problem: it reveals the emergence of
polity,   and   with   it   the   need   to   understand   policy   as   constitutional

The   third   argued   that   once   polity   appears,   citizenship   becomes

This fourth document takes the next step. It stops describing the order
and begins constituting it.
That move is necessary because logos and mythos are not enough. Logos
can explain the system. Mythos can make it inhabitable. But without
ethos, logos becomes procedural and mythos becomes manipulable. A
constitution is how a polity binds itself to principles that limit power,
preserve dignity, and define legitimate coexistence.
That need is especially acute in the agentic internet. The central question
is no longer simply how to govern software systems. It is how humans and
agents   are   to   coexist   within   a   lawful   order   in   which   autonomy,
stewardship,   accountability,   and   rights   protection   are   increasingly
mediated through machine-extended systems.
The same agents that may protect children, guard enterprise assets, detect
masquerade,   escalate   harms,   and   uphold   rights   may   also   become
instruments   of   over-administration,   surveillance,   coercion,   and
institutional overreach if they are not constitutionally restrained. Their
promise is precisely what makes their limitation necessary.
This   Constitution   therefore   begins   from   a   non-negotiable   premise:
personhood precedes permission.

the agentic polity.
We affirm that personhood precedes permission, that dignity precedes
administration, and that foundational rights are not granted by
platforms, institutions, or states, but recognized as inalienable.
We establish this Constitution to order coexistence among humans,
personas, institutions, and agents within a lawful, accountable, and
rights-preserving digital polity.
We bind governance to stewardship rather than domination, policy to
constitutional principle rather than arbitrary control, and delegated
power to auditable responsibility rather than opaque autonomy.

with international human rights law.
Section 8.  Constitutional protection may be delegated, but constitutional

Article XII — Amendment and Constitutional
Restraint
Section 1. This Constitution may be amended only through processes that
preserve   its   integrity,   its   foundational   hierarchy   of   rights,   and   its
compatibility with international human rights law.
Section 2. No amendment shall be valid if it purports to negate the dignity of
the sovereign natural person, the priority of personhood over permission,
the foundational rights floor, the subordination of policy to constitutional
order,   or   the   constitutional   requirement   that   delegated   power   remain
bounded, disclosed, auditable, and reviewable.
Section 3. Amendments affecting foundational rights, personhood anchors,
standing, constitutional enforcement, or the relationship between polity and
citizen shall require heightened review and a supermajoritarian threshold
appropriate to constitutional gravity.
Section 4. Amendments affecting domain-specific governance, institutional
procedures, or implementation details may be subject to lower thresholds,
provided   that   such   changes   do   not   indirectly   erode   the   entrenched
principles named in Section 2.

Section 5.  All   proposed   amendments   shall   be   published   with   sufficient
notice, interpretive explanation, and opportunity for review, challenge, and
public constitutional scrutiny.
Section 6. The polity shall maintain a constitutional review function capable
of assessing whether proposed amendments, policies, delegated systems, or
institutional orders remain compatible with the foundational principles and
rights floor of this Constitution.
Section 7. No amendment shall acquire legitimacy solely by administrative
enactment,   market   dominance,   technical   inevitability,   or   platform
convenience.
Section 8.  Reference implementations, open standards, and institutional
practices may evolve, but such evolution shall not be construed as self-
authorizing constitutional amendment.
Section 9. Constitutional restraint shall be understood not as resistance to
adaptation, but as the disciplined preservation of those principles without
which adaptation would become arbitrary rule.
Section 10. This Constitution shall remain amendable in form, but bound in
spirit to the protection of the sovereign natural person, the legitimacy of
standing, the boundedness of delegated power, and the possibility of just
coexistence between humans and agents.
“Reference implementations may lead, but they may not monopolize
constitutional legitimacy.”

Glossary
TermWorking definition
Foundational rights
Rights that attach to the sovereign natural person by virtue of
proven personhood and that precede citizenship, role, or
membership.
Civic rights
Rights that arise from standing within the polity, including
participation, appeal, representation, and contribution.
Institutional rights
Rights that arise from collective standing, including
governance, continuity, stewardship, and treasury.
Delegated rights
Rights or authorities exercised on behalf of another subject or
domain under bounded scope and review.
KybeDIDs
Canonical proof-of-personhood anchors used to make
foundational constitutional rights operational without
collapsing the person into everyday exposure.
DiDQubes
Governable identity containers and disclosure surfaces through
which standing, representation, delegation, and contextual
participation are exercised.

Appendices
Appendix A — Constitutional Design Principles
Personhood precedes permission.
Foundational rights precede citizenship.
Citizenship precedes delegated power.
Policy is subordinate to constitutional order.
Stewardship must not extinguish autonomy.
Agents may support constitutional protection only under disclosed, bounded,
auditable, and reviewable authority.
Appendix B — Operating Model
Open constitutional standards may be stewarded through public-interest
structures such as the Internet Foundation.
AgentiQ OS may serve as an open operating substrate.
metaProof may provide reference implementations of the AgentiQ Platform
and metaMe Runtime.
Other entities may provide compliant KybeDID systems, identity layers,
constitutional runtimes, stewardship agents, and trust layers provided they
conform to constitutional standards.
