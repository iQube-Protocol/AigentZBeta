Citizenship in the Agentic Internet
Rights, Duties, Standing, and Belonging in a Human–Agent Polity
Abstract
Once   the   internet   becomes   governable   as   a   polity,   the   next   unavoidable   question   is
citizenship. Citizenship is not reducible to identity or access. It is the civic standing by which
subjects hold rights, duties, legitimacy, and participation within a constitutional order. In the
agentic internet, that question becomes more complex because the polity includes not only
natural persons and institutions, but personas, delegated identities, agents, and synthetic
actors operating across physical and digital domains.
This paper argues that digital citizenship must be built on a prior human-rights floor. The
agentic polity must not begin from platform permission, institutional convenience, or state
recognition.   It   must   begin   from   the   inalienable   rights   of   the   sovereign   natural   person.
Statelessness makes this principle concrete: UNHCR reported 4.4 million stateless people or
people   of   undetermined   nationality   at   mid-2025,   while   warning   that   the   true   number   is
higher   because   many   states   do   not   report   statelessness   data.   Article   15   of   the   Universal
Declaration of Human Rights affirms that everyone has the right to a nationality and must
not be arbitrarily deprived of it.
The paper therefore proposes that proof of personhood becomes the constitutional key that
unlocks  foundational rights in  the  digital  polity, and that  KybeDIDs serve as the deepest
technical anchors by which that proof is made operational. On top of that floor, the paper
distinguishes   identity,   membership,   standing,   and   citizenship;   classifies   rights   into
foundational,   civic,   institutional,   and   delegated   forms;   and   examines   how   bounded
autonomy and delegated stewardship operate across family, enterprise, and agentic contexts.
Youth digital harms and age-bounded legislation make these questions urgent, but they do
not exhaust them. The deeper concern is how humans and agents are to coexist within a
legitimate constitutional order.

I. From Governance to Belonging
A constitution without citizens is only half a polity.
That sentence is no longer philosophical ornament. Digital environments now shape identity,
aspiration, attention, reputation, belonging, memory, and risk at civilizational scale. In the
United States, suicide was the second leading cause of death among people ages 10–34 in
2023. Globally, suicide was the third leading cause of death among 15–29-year-olds in 2021.
The U.S. Surgeon General has warned that the present evidence does not support concluding
that social media is sufficiently safe for children and adolescents. These facts do not prove a
single   simple   causal   story.   They   do   prove   that   digital   environments   now   carry   civic
consequences too serious to be treated as secondary product-design issues.
Recent legal and policy developments reinforce the point. In March 2026, a Los Angeles jury
found   Meta   and   YouTube   negligent   in   a   landmark   youth   social-media-addiction   case.   In
December 2025, Australia’s under-16 social-media ban took effect. The Australian Human
Rights   Commission,   meanwhile,   argued   that   any   such   ban   must   satisfy   necessity   and
proportionality   if   it   is   to   remain   compatible   with   children’s   rights.   Together,   these
developments show that age-bounded digital participation is no longer being treated solely
as a private product decision. It is now a matter of public governance.
Young people provide one of the clearest and most urgent contexts in which the civic failures
of   digital   life   become   visible.   But   the   question   this   paper   asks   is   broader:   not   only   how
minors are protected, but how standing, rights, duties, delegation, and legitimacy are to be
organized across a mixed society of humans, institutions, personas, and agents.
The most sensitive boundary in this whole framework is the relationship between polity and
citizen. That is where digital order can become emancipatory, protective, and sovereignty-
enhancing. It is also where it can become authoritarian, administrative, and rights-eroding. A
policy-rich polity can easily become a machine for conditioning participation if it lacks a
prior rights floor that it is forbidden to cross.
This paper argues that such a floor must exist, and that it must begin not from platform
membership or state recognition, but from personhood.
II. Why Identity Is Not Enough
Identity is necessary. It is not enough. Most digital systems still treat identity as though it
were the primary civic question. Who is this user? Is the credential valid? Has the account
been authenticated? These are important questions, but they are not the same as asking who
or what has legitimate standing in the polity.

Citizenship answers the highest civic question: what standing, rights, duties, and forms of
participation that subject legitimately holds within the order of the polity.
This   is   why   identity   in   a   civic   system   is   not   only   proof   of   self.   It   is   proof   of   legitimate
presence, developmental fit, and bounded eligibility. A mature digital polity must be able to
prove not only that someone is old enough to enter certain spaces, but that they are young
enough   to   belong   in   others   —   and   that   those   who   stand   among   them   do   so   only   under
legitimate, auditable authority.

This   opens   a   much   richer   role   for   DiDQubes   than   conventional   age   verification   or   login

role, and contextual belonging. They can help answer questions like these: Is this actor part

These are not moderation questions in the narrow sense. They are questions of standing. One
of   the   defining   governance   failures   of   digital   society   has   been   the   inability   to   prevent
humans  from  masquerading   as  who  they   are  not   in  spaces   where  vulnerability   matters.
Adults   can   masquerade   as   children.   Predators   can   masquerade   as   peers.   Bad   actors   can
masquerade as trusted participants. Agents can masquerade as humans without adequate
disclosure. Humans can deploy agents under ambiguous authority. The failure to distinguish
identity   from   eligibility   has   left   vulnerable   populations   exposed   to   actors   who   had   no
legitimate standing among them.
Identity in a civic system is not only proof of self. It is proof of legitimate
presence, developmental fit, and bounded eligibility.
III. The Human-Rights Floor
The agentic internet is global by nature. If it becomes a polity in any meaningful sense, it
cannot rely only on state citizenship as the basis of standing. It must ask a more fundamental
question: what rights attach to the sovereign natural person before any state, platform, or
institution recognizes them?
This   is   where   statelessness   becomes   constitutionally   decisive.   UNHCR   reported   that   4.4
million   people   were   stateless   or   of   undetermined   nationality   at   mid-2025,   but   also
emphasized   that   the   true   number   is   higher   because   many   countries   do   not   report
statelessness   data   at   all.   Stateless   persons   are   often   denied   access   to   documentation,
education,   health   care,   work,   marriage,   and   freedom   of   movement.   Article   15   of   the
Universal Declaration of Human Rights matters here because it states that everyone has the
right to a nationality and that no one shall be arbitrarily deprived of it. The digital polity
cannot   solve   statelessness   in   the   legal   sense   on   its   own,   but   it   reveals   something
foundational: when civic recognition fails, personhood must not.
This is the human-rights floor of the agentic polity. The floor is not platform membership. It
is not institutional standing. It is not state convenience. It is not administrative permission. It
is the inalienable human rights of the sovereign natural person.

The stateless person is therefore not an exception to digital polity. They are its constitutional
test. If the polity cannot preserve the rights floor of the sovereign natural person even where
conventional citizenship fails, then it is not a legitimate constitutional order. It has become
only an administrative machine.
This is where proof of personhood becomes the constitutional key. The polity must not claim
the right to create personhood. It must instead be able to recognize, attest, and operationalize
it. KybeDIDs are critical because they provide the deepest technical anchors through which
proof of personhood can be established. They are not everyday working identities. They are
canonical   anchors   of   personhood   itself.   On   top   of   that   anchor,   Root   DIDs   and   Root   DID

without   unnecessary   exposure.   DiDQubes   then   provide   the   governable   containers   and
disclosure   surfaces   through   which   identity,   delegation,   representation,   and   participation
become contextually legible.

The governing principle is simple: foundational rights cannot legitimately be reduced by
platform rules, institutional convenience, or state hostility. They sit below and prior to the
citizenship layer. Citizenship extends from that floor. It does not replace it.
That matters because it is exactly at the polity–citizen boundary that authoritarian abuse can
hide. If the polity is allowed to decide who counts as fully human by purely administrative
criteria, then the whole framework becomes dangerous. If, by contrast, personhood is prior,
and   proof   of   personhood   unlocks   a   constitutional   floor   that   the   polity   itself   may   not
undercut, then the framework gains a real safeguard against rights erosion.
IV. A Civic Taxonomy of Standing
The polity contains different classes of subjects and actors, and they do not all occupy the
same civic position.
The sovereign natural person remains the deepest subject. This is the bearer of foundational
rights and the ultimate source of dignity, accountability, and legitimacy.
The persona is a contextual civic surface. It may carry reputation, role-specific standing,
public-facing obligations, and rights expression. It does not exhaust the sovereign subject
behind it.
The institution is a collective or juridical subject. It can hold continuity, treasury, governance,
and delegated authority across time. Its standing is real, but it is not the same as natural
personhood.

The agent is more complex. Agents are not citizens by default. They may function as tools,
delegates, bounded representatives, or constrained civic actors under charter. Their standing
is derivative, scoped, and governed.
This is the distinction the paper must preserve. It is not arguing for indiscriminate agent
rights. It is arguing for differentiated standing inside a legitimate order. Not every actor is a
citizen, and not every citizen is a direct actor.
V. Bounded Autonomy and Delegated Stewardship
Citizenship without duty collapses into consumption.
That line matters because digital life has often been organized around entitlement without
stewardship.   If   the   next   era   is   to   be   a   polity   rather   than   a   marketplace   with   better
permissions, duty has to return to the center.
The   strongest   way   to   frame   this   is   through   a   general   constitutional   pattern:   bounded
autonomy under delegated stewardship. A subject has real privacy, agency, discretion, and
developmental or professional room to act. But another legitimate actor may hold bounded
responsibility to protect, supervise, or govern the conditions of that participation within a
shared domain.
This pattern appears across parent and child, caregiver and ward, institution and member,
company and employee, and human principal and delegated agent. These are not identical
relationships.   But   they   pose   the   same   structural   question:   how   to   preserve   legitimate
autonomy   without   collapsing   governance,   and   how   to   enforce   governance   without
extinguishing autonomy.
This is why the youth case and the enterprise case belong in the same paper. An employee is
not   a   child.   But   neither   is   an   employee   a   totally   unconstrained   sovereign   in   relation   to
enterprise assets. They have real autonomy, judgment, and privacy interests. Yet they also
act within fiduciary and policy constraints, with responsibilities toward assets they do not
personally   own.   The   original   spark   for   this   series   —   the   Claude   Code   leak   —   is   best
understood in this light. It was not just a packaging error. It was an instance of insufficiently
governed autonomy in relation to sensitive enterprise assets.
The constitutional challenge is therefore not whether subjects should have autonomy, but
how autonomy is bounded, stewarded, and made accountable within shared domains.

VI. Delegation, Representation, and metaMe Runtime
The agentic internet is a delegated society. Humans act through personas, roles, institutions,
and increasingly through agents. Institutions act through employees, systems, and delegated
machine processes. Parents act on behalf of children. Companies govern access to enterprise
assets through employees who are neither mere subjects nor unconstrained sovereigns. The
constitutional question is therefore not whether delegation exists. It is how delegation is
bounded, legitimized, and held accountable.
This is where metaMe Runtime becomes especially important. metaMe Runtime should not
be understood only as a personal runtime. It can also function as a constitutional runtime
through   which   sovereign   control,   delegated   stewardship,   institutional   policy,   and   agentic
delegation are all expressed.
In a sovereign mode, it gives adult individuals a way to hold and govern their own runtime.
In   a   guardianship   mode,   it   allows   parents,   caregivers,   or   lawful   guardians   to   delegate
bounded protective authority into the runtime of a minor. This matters because it creates a
way to protect young people in distributed digital spaces without collapsing them into total
visibility or total parental domination. The challenge is not simply to protect the young, but
to   do   so   without   extinguishing   the   privacy,   developmental   agency,   and   dignity   through
which healthy citizenship must be formed.

In   an   institutional   mode,   it   allows   enterprises   to   set   policy   boundaries   around   assets,
disclosures, and delegations within employee-facing work environments, without pretending
that every worker must either be fully distrusted or fully unconstrained. In a delegation
mode, it allows humans and institutions to authorize agents under explicit scope, role, and
accountability rules.
This is where guardian agents become especially powerful. In youth settings, agents may
serve as bounded protective delegates: auditing protected spaces for age-cohort mismatches,
detecting   patterns   consistent   with   grooming   or   masquerade,   enforcing   cohort-bounded
communication rules, escalating suspicious behavior to verified guardians or moderators,
and generating receipts for intervention and review. In enterprise settings, analogous agents
may   monitor   policy   violations,   risky   movement   of   crown-jewel   assets,   or   unauthorized
disclosure paths.
The first civic role of agents in the polity may therefore be not expansive rights-claims, but
stewardship and guardianship under human authority. Before the polity asks what rights
agents may hold, it must ask how vulnerable humans are shielded from those who have no
right to stand among them.
The significance of metaMe Runtime, then, is that it becomes a live constitutional substrate
for governed autonomy. It provides a way to hold together what digital systems too often
split apart: privacy and protection, freedom and stewardship, discretion and accountability,
developmental becoming and bounded oversight.

VII. KNYT and Civic Formation
Citizenship is not only classified. It is formed.
A polity does not endure merely because it has rules. It endures because subjects learn how
to   belong   inside   it.   They   learn   role,   consequence,   aspiration,   stewardship,   restraint,
responsibility,   participation,   and   memory.   Those   things   are   rarely   taught   first   through
abstract institutional language. They are often taught through story, symbol, ritual, role, and
world-building. That is why mythos matters.
This becomes even more important when the subjects of civic formation are young people
growing up native to the agentic internet. Their first lessons in belonging, status, aspiration,
and   consequence   are   increasingly   mediated   through   digital   environments.   The   question,

This is why KNYT matters. KNYT should not be understood as a decorative narrative wrapper

KNYT can therefore be framed as a mythic civic environment in which younger participants

delegation and agency, reward and consequence, progression and belonging, and boundaries
and stewardship.
That does not mean KNYT replaces civic institutions or formal constitutional order. It means
it prepares subjects to inhabit them. It makes digital citizenship narratable before it becomes
fully   formalized.   When   digital   platforms   already   influence   the   mental,   social,   and
aspirational development of the young at scale, the relevant question is no longer whether
story-worlds shape them. The relevant question is whether those worlds are governed by
extractive attention logic or by a more serious civic grammar.

Conclusion
The language of “user” is too thin for the next era.
Governance requires subjects, not merely consumers. Youth safety, enterprise governance,
and delegated agentic action all make the stakes vivid, but the question is larger than any
one of those examples. The real question is how humans and agents are to coexist within a
legitimate constitutional order that preserves personhood, differentiates standing, structures
stewardship, and resists authoritarian reduction.
The polity does not grant personhood. It recognizes it, attests it, and builds civic standing on
top of it. The human-rights floor of the sovereign natural person must come first. Citizenship
must come after that, not before it. And if the polity is global by nature, then the rights of
