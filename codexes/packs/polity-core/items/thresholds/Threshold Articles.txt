The Missing Primitive of the AI Revolution: What OpenClaw Taught Us About Digital Representation
For the past three years, the AI race has been defined by one question.
How intelligent is it?
Every new model was measured by how well it reasoned, coded, wrote, analysed and solved increasingly complex problems. The competition became one of capability. Every benchmark promised another step towards Artificial General Intelligence.
Then, over the past six months, something changed.
The biggest story in AI wasn’t another model release.
It wasn’t another benchmark.
It wasn’t another reasoning breakthrough.
It was OpenClaw.
Not because OpenClaw was simply another impressive AI system, but because it fundamentally changed the question we were asking.
For the first time, millions of people stopped asking:
“Can AI do this?”
and began asking:
“Can AI do this for me?”
That distinction changes everything.
It marks the transition from intelligence to agency.
People no longer want AI that simply answers questions. They want AI that books flights, negotiates contracts, pays invoices, manages projects, coordinates teams, purchases goods, interacts with governments and represents them throughout an increasingly digital economy.
OpenClaw didn’t simply demonstrate better AI.
It validated something much more important.
It proved that people want digital representatives.
And in doing so, it exposed the next great challenge of the AI revolution.
Not intelligence.
Not even agency.
But legitimacy.
The Mac Mini Gold Rush
One of the most fascinating consequences of OpenClaw wasn’t software.
It was hardware.
Almost overnight, Mac minis became one of the most sought-after computers in technology circles.
People weren’t buying them because they suddenly needed more computing power.
They were buying them because they wanted somewhere “safe” to run their AI representatives.
That behaviour tells us something profound.
People aren’t afraid of AI.
They’re afraid of giving AI authority.
Running an AI on a dedicated machine feels reassuring.
It feels contained.
It feels responsible.
But it reminds me of another moment in history.
Whenever confidence in banks begins to falter, people withdraw cash and hide it under the mattress.
It feels safer.
But hiding money under the mattress isn’t a banking system.
Likewise, running an AI on its own Mac mini isn’t a governance system.
It’s an understandable response to uncertainty.
But it doesn’t solve the real problem.
Because the problem isn’t where your representative runs.
The problem is what your representative is authorised to do.
We’ve Been Solving the Wrong Problem
Much of today’s conversation is about infrastructure.
Should agents run locally?
Should they run in the cloud?
Should they be sandboxed?
Should they have unrestricted internet access?
These are important engineering questions.
But they are not the defining question.
The defining question is remarkably simple.
Under whose authority is this representative acting?
And equally important:
What are the limits of that authority?
Today, most AI systems operate on an all-or-nothing permission model.
Either they have access to your email.
Or they don’t.
Either they can spend your money.
Or they can’t.
Either they can access your files.
Or they cannot.
Human society has never operated like this.
Neither should AI.
We’ve spent the last six months trying to contain AI representatives.
The next decade will be about governing digital representation.
The first generation of agent platforms is trying to secure computers.
The next generation will secure authority.
Civilisation Solved This Problem Centuries Ago

It’s one civilisation solved centuries ago.
Lawyers represent clients.
Doctors represent patients.

Directors represent shareholders.
Diplomats represent nations.
Trustees represent beneficiaries.

Their authority is delegated.
It has purpose.
It has boundaries.

It has jurisdictions.
It has time limits.
It can be revoked.
Most importantly, everyone else can verify those limits before accepting the actions of the representative.
Representation has always depended on governance.
For some reason, we’ve forgotten that lesson just as AI becomes capable of representing us.
Intelligence. Agency. Representation.

Can machines think?

Can machines act?

Can machines legitimately represent us?

Representation requires authority.

Representation requires governance.
Representation requires evidence.
Without those four things, an AI may be capable.
It cannot be legitimate.
The Missing Primitive
The missing primitive of the Agentic Internet isn’t another model.
It isn’t another benchmark.
It isn’t another context window.
It is bounded delegation.
Bounded delegation allows an individual, organisation or government to create an authorised digital representative with explicitly defined powers.
A representative may negotiate software contracts.
Purchase goods below an agreed financial threshold.
Book international travel.
Renew insurance.
Submit regulatory filings.
Access healthcare information for a defined purpose.

Every permission is explicit.
Every permission is bounded.
Every permission is revocable.
Every permission is independently verifiable.
That changes the relationship between people and AI.
From Trusting Platforms to Governing Representation

Trust the AI provider.
Trust the operating system.
Trust the cloud.
Trust the infrastructure.
But trust doesn’t scale.
Governance does.
The future of AI won’t be built on trust.

Policy determines what a representative may do.
Who it may represent.
Under what authority.
Within what financial limits.
Under which jurisdictions.
Requiring which approvals.
Meeting which Standing requirements.
Protecting which information.
The representative doesn’t invent those rules.
Its principal does.
The representative simply carries them.

The Internet Needs a Constitution
For most of its history, the Internet has been described as infrastructure.
A network.
A communications system.
A collection of protocols.
An extraordinary engineering achievement.
All of those descriptions remain true.
But they are no longer sufficient.
Because somewhere along the way, without quite noticing, we stopped building a network.
We built a society.
That is the threshold we’ve crossed.
And societies require constitutions.
We Built a Network. We Got a Society.
The Internet is arguably humanity’s greatest collaborative engineering achievement.
It connected continents.
Democratised publishing.
Created entirely new economies.
Enabled billions of people to communicate, create and collaborate in ways previous generations could scarcely imagine.
Its architects solved one of history’s great technical challenges:
How do you reliably move information across a global network?
The protocols they created answered that question brilliantly.
But they were never intended to answer another.
How do you govern a society?
Because thirty years ago, the Internet wasn’t one.
Today, increasingly, it is.
We work there.
We trade there.
We learn there.
We build communities there.
We create identities there.
We establish reputations there.
We earn our living there.
We conduct commerce there.
Increasingly, we don’t simply use the Internet.
We live through it.
We didn’t build the Internet to become a society.
It became one anyway.
Code Became Law
Constitutions rarely appear by accident.
When constitutional principles are absent, something else inevitably fills the vacuum.
On today’s Internet, that something became software.
Without consciously deciding to, we allowed platforms to assume constitutional roles.
They increasingly determine:
Who may participate.
What may be said.
How identity is established.
How reputation is measured.
How commerce takes place.
Who is visible.
Who is discoverable.
Who is excluded.
These are constitutional functions.
